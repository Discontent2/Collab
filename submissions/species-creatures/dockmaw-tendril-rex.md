## One-Paragraph Summary

The **Dockmaw Tendril-Rex** is a small-vehicle-sized roadside scavenger that lives in dry canyons and cliff-cut route corridors, where it clings to rock faces and abandoned structures using long anchoring tendrils. Its broad armored skull, heavy chest, and reduced forebody give it a silhouette that makes witnesses compare it to a tyrannosaur crossed with a grounded aircraft fuselage. Covered in layered shell armor and cable-like appendages, it often perches motionless above canyon roads, tasting mineral dust and windborne grit until prey, salvage, or convoy vibration passes below.

## Visual Description

A massive saurian-adjacent body with a deep blunt T. rex-like skull, thick armored neck, powerful torso, and long balancing tail. The back and flanks are covered in shell plates that resemble overlapping hull panels, worn armor shingles, or bio-mechanical fuselage plating. Along the ribs, shoulders, and hips hang retractable tendon-cables and tendrils that anchor into canyon walls, bridge undersides, ruined gantries, and cliff shelves. At rest, it can look like a crashed aircraft nose or stripped industrial glider body fused to a living predator.

## Habitat

Dry canyons, road cuts, abandoned cliffside stations, eroded bridge spans, canyon salvage corridors, derelict World Works route sites, and sun-blasted ravines with old support structures.

## Behavior

The Dockmaw Tendril-Rex is a patient ambush scavenger and route hazard. Rather than roaming constantly, it anchors itself to rock, concrete, or scrap and extends tendrils outward to brace its body. It waits, tastes the air and dust for mineral signatures, and tracks movement through canyon echo and substrate vibration. It may drop from a wall, swing into a lane, or rake cargo with hooked tendrils.

## Relationship To People

This species is frequently misclassified as collapsed aircraft wreck, inactive route drone husk, canyon safety fixture, derelict World Works support organism, armored contamination fauna, or fixed hazard object rather than living animal. Locals and route workers watch for anchor scars, stripped bones below ledges, and dead wreckage that changes position between shifts.

## Weakness / Non-Combat Handling

Do not approach wreckage hanging unnaturally above roads. Watch for anchor tendrils in rock faces. Avoid passing directly beneath it. Use noise diversion or thrown mineral scrap away from the route. Do not strip armor plates while it is alive. If it commits too many tendrils to one wall position, it becomes slower to reorient.
