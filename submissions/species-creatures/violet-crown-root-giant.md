## Backstory

The first reliable report of the **Violet Crown Root-Giant** came from a World Works recovery crew sent to survey a failed wetland restoration patch near a forgotten route spur. The job looked simple on paper: catalogue overgrowth, mark salvageable water-control equipment, pull sensor stakes, return before dark.

Only one worker came back with the field tablet.

The tablet footage showed a forest that did not match any official planting record. Purple fungal shelves climbed every trunk. Vines hung in layered curtains. Shallow turquoise water moved through old machine ruts, but the water flowed uphill in places, as if roots below the mud were pulling it by memory rather than gravity.

At the center of the wetland stood what the crew first logged as a dead tree.

Then the “tree” opened its eyes.

The Violet Crown Root-Giant is not believed to be a single creature in the normal sense. It may be a walking colony: tree-body, fungal crown, root-muscle, swamp bacteria, drowned machine minerals, and scavenged animal instinct braided into one enormous mobile ecology. Its face appears carved from bark and bone-like mycelium, but local witnesses insist the face changes depending on who sees it. A frightened worker sees a monster. A lost child sees a guardian. A guilty trespasser sees a judge made of rot.

## Origin Rumor

No safe record confirms where it came from.

There are three common theories, all unverified:

- World Works calls it an **unplanned bloom outcome**, probably caused by contaminated restoration material and abandoned bio-survey equipment.
- Local travelers say it grew around a collapsed refuge marker and learned to hate false safety.
- Antisapian storytellers describe it as a **hungry garden that remembers every name whispered into wet soil**, but they do not treat that as a literal explanation. More like a warning with teeth.

None of these accounts should be treated as canon truth.

## Behavior

The Root-Giant moves slowly, but the forest around it moves fast.

Its actual footsteps are rare. More often, it changes the environment before changing position:

- roots rise like knuckles under the mud
- mushrooms bloom in fresh footprints
- vines tighten around old metal
- fallen logs rotate to block paths
- pools brighten when it listens
- insects go silent before it turns its head

It feeds on rot, mineral runoff, dead wood, old machine residue, and carrion. It does not appear to hunt people by default. Instead, it reacts to intrusion, loud machinery, cutting tools, fire, and attempts to drain or map its wetland.

If approached quietly, it may simply watch.

If harmed, the entire swamp becomes its mouth.

## Relationship To People

Travelers fear it, but some communities leave offerings at the edge of its wetland: broken tool handles, spoiled fruit, spent batteries, carved wood charms, rusted survey tags, and bowls of clean water.

World Works wants samples of the violet crown fungus.

Black-market collectors sell fake “crown caps,” claiming they can cure rot-sickness, predict safe water, or make machines grow roots. Most are ordinary mushrooms dyed purple.

Aeonolacertian route-keepers would likely read the Root-Giant as a threshold warning: a place where the land has stopped being scenery and become host. Their safest interpretation might be:

**Do not enter a living wetland as cargo. Enter as a guest, or not at all.**

## Story Use

The Violet Crown Root-Giant works best as a **moral encounter**, not just a monster fight.

It can guard:

- a drowned route station
- a false sanctuary ruin
- an old water-control site
- a lost field recorder
- a hidden survivor camp
- a place where machinery harmed the land
- a clue wrapped in vines but not explained

It should create pressure near sanctuary and Worldskin themes without solving them. The creature may suggest that nature remembers institutional harm, but it should not reveal protected mechanisms.

## Encounter Seed

A crew enters a flooded forest to recover a sealed World Works case. The case is visible, hanging from roots inside the Root-Giant’s chest cavity like a swallowed bell.

The Root-Giant does not attack.

It waits.

The real challenge is deciding what the case is worth, whether it belongs to the company that lost it, and whether cutting it free would wake the whole wetland.

## Non-Combat Handling

Safe approaches include:

- lowering tools
- shutting off engines
- avoiding fire
- stepping only on exposed stone or deadfall
- offering clean water or non-toxic metal
- refusing to cut living roots
- speaking softly and leaving clear paths
- retreating when mushrooms close their caps

Violence should make things worse. The Root-Giant is too connected to its habitat to treat as a single target.
