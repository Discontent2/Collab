## One-Paragraph Summary

The **Ruin-Shell Basilisk** is a human-sized saurian pseudo-organism that inhabits industrial wreckage, route debris fields, and abandoned work sites by nesting inside discarded exo-frames, maintenance shells, broken biosuits, and labor hardware. It is frequently mistaken for inactive equipment because its body folds into a rigid, wearable-looking crouch, its skin resembles layered composite plating, and its faint static halo reads like damaged power bleed. It is not a true machine, but a living scavenger-adapted creature whose survival strategy depends on being ignored, mishandled, or officially misclassified.

## Visual Description

A lean, basilisk-like body with a narrow wedge head, folded forelimbs, backward-jointed hind legs, and a long balancing tail. Its hide looks like a fusion of reptile tissue and worn industrial shell: matte plates, seam-like muscle bands, cable-like tendons, and translucent patches where dim static pulses under the skin. When at rest, it tucks itself into the silhouette of an empty exosuit or kneeling utility rig. The eyes are small and reflective, often hidden beneath visor-like brow plating. Along the spine and collar line are ribbed structures that resemble harness mounts or suit clasps, reinforcing the illusion that it is equipment.

## Habitat

Most often associated with:

- industrial ruins
- abandoned route stations
- salvage yards
- equipment graveyards
- labor camps gone quiet
- partially stripped World Works recovery zones

It prefers places where tools, suit frames, brackets, cables, and metal carcasses accumulate. It uses such environments as camouflage and shelter.

## Behavior

The Ruin-Shell Basilisk is not an aggressive apex hunter. It is a patient opportunist. It nests in or beneath discarded machinery, absorbs warmth from metal surfaces, and scavenges trace minerals, oils, fungus growth, insects, and small ruin-dwelling fauna. It moves cautiously between cover, often dragging or wearing fragments of broken structure that make it look even more like abandoned gear.

Its static halo is subtle but noticeable in the dark: a dry crackle around the body, tiny arc-like flickers along shell edges, and hair-raising interference near close observers. This may be used for communication, intimidation, or environmental sensing.

## Relationship To People

This creature has a strong labor-culture relationship. Workers, scavengers, route crews, and salvage runners are the people most likely to encounter it. It becomes dangerous mainly when mistaken for a usable machine, suit, or recoverable asset.

Common relationship patterns:

- workers think it is discarded equipment
- salvagers try to haul it away
- mechanics find it nesting inside dead machinery
- local crews trade stories about “the suit that blinks back”
- authorities may log it incorrectly as damaged field hardware or contaminated gear
- experienced travelers sometimes read its presence as a sign that a site has been undisturbed for a long time

## Encounter Use

Possible encounter patterns:

- A crew tries to recover an “abandoned exosuit,” only to realize it is alive.
- A ruin contains several “empty suits,” but only one is a basilisk and the rest are real scrap.
- A traveler follows static interference expecting machinery and instead finds a nesting site.
- A mechanic discovers that repeated damage reports were actually caused by these creatures sheltering inside equipment housings.
- A route camp treats the creature as a nuisance until it is revealed to be feeding on vermin or warning of unstable ruin sections.

## Weakness / Non-Combat Handling

Best handled non-violently.

Effective approaches include:

- backing away and not surrounding it
- reducing bright direct inspection lights
- leaving it a clear path to retreat
- avoiding attempts to “help” by lifting, harnessing, or activating it
- luring it away with warmth, vibration, or scrap piles rather than force
- marking nesting zones so labor crews stop mistaking them for salvage caches

It is vulnerable mainly to overexposure, stress, and habitat disruption. Mishandling by corporations or overzealous salvage programs is likely more dangerous than the creature itself.
